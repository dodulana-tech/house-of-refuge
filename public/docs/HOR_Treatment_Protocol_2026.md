## 2. GLOBAL EVIDENCE BASE

This section presents the international research foundations upon which every clinical decision in this protocol rests. HOR does not adopt practices because they are traditional or convenient; every intervention is selected because systematic research evidence supports its efficacy for substance use disorder populations.

### 2.1 NIDA's 13 Principles of Effective Drug Addiction Treatment

The National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA), a division of the US National Institutes of Health, published its Principles of Drug Addiction Treatment after more than two decades of funded clinical research. These 13 principles represent the most widely cited evidence-based framework for addiction treatment programme design globally. HOR's protocol is built to satisfy every principle:

**Principle 1: No single treatment is appropriate for everyone.** Treatment must be matched to the individual's particular problems and needs. HOR implements this through the ASAM six-dimensional assessment at admission (Section 5), individualised treatment planning using the Columbia Model (Section 8), and bi-weekly plan reviews at Multi-Disciplinary Team meetings.

**Principle 2: Treatment needs to be readily available.** Because drug-addicted individuals may be uncertain about entering treatment, taking advantage of available services the moment people are ready is critical. HOR maintains an open referral pathway with same-week admission capacity and an Outpatient Engagement Pathway (Section 4.5) for clients not yet ready for residential care, ensuring no potential client is turned away without a service offer.

**Principle 3: Effective treatment attends to multiple needs of the individual, not just drug abuse.** To be effective, treatment must address the individual's drug abuse and any associated medical, psychological, social, vocational, and legal problems. HOR's treatment model integrates clinical therapy, psychiatric screening, spiritual formation, vocational rehabilitation, family systems work, and community reintegration planning as concurrent streams (Section 6).

**Principle 4: An individual's treatment and services plan must be assessed continually and modified as necessary.** A client may require varying combinations of services during the course of treatment and recovery. HOR mandates treatment plan reviews at Week 4, Week 8, and pre-discharge, with emergency reviews triggered by clinical events (Section 8.1).

**Principle 5: Remaining in treatment for an adequate period of time is critical.** Research indicates that most addicted individuals need at least three months in treatment to significantly reduce or stop their drug use, and that the best outcomes occur with longer durations. HOR's 12-week (84-day) minimum programme duration is directly derived from this evidence, further supported by De Leon's Therapeutic Community research showing 9x better outcomes for clients retained beyond 90 days (De Leon, 2000) and CBT meta-analytic evidence identifying a critical window for skill consolidation within three months post-treatment initiation (PMC10572095, 2023).

**Principle 6: Behavioral therapies -- including individual, family, or group counselling -- are the most commonly used forms of drug abuse treatment.** Behavioral therapies vary in their focus and may involve addressing motivation to change, building skills to resist drug use, replacing drug-using activities with constructive ones, and improving problem-solving skills. HOR delivers Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT), Motivational Interviewing (MI), relapse prevention therapy, and family systems therapy as core interventions (Sections 8, 9, 14).

**Principle 7: Medications are an important element of treatment for many patients, especially when combined with counselling and other behavioral therapies.** HOR integrates pharmacological management for withdrawal (Section 7), co-occurring psychiatric conditions (Section 12), and symptomatic relief, all under physician supervision and in accordance with WHO Essential Medicines List recommendations.

**Principle 8: An individual's treatment and services plan must be assessed continually and modified as necessary to ensure it meets changing needs.** This principle reinforces Principle 4 and is operationalised through HOR's MDT review schedule, real-time clinical observations documented in progress notes, and the flexibility to extend treatment duration to 26 weeks where clinically indicated.

**Principle 9: Many drug-addicted individuals also have other mental disorders.** Because substance use disorders and other mental illnesses are often co-occurring, clients presenting for either condition should be assessed for the co-occurrence of the other. HOR administers the PHQ-9 (depression), GAD-7 (anxiety), and targeted trauma screening at admission, consistent with WHO mhGAP (2023) recommendations for integrated mental health and substance use assessment in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs).

**Principle 10: Medically assisted detoxification is only the first stage of addiction treatment and by itself does little to change long-term drug abuse.** Although medically assisted detoxification can safely manage acute withdrawal symptoms, it is rarely sufficient to help addicted individuals achieve long-term abstinence. HOR's detoxification phase (Weeks 1-2) is explicitly positioned as the gateway to therapeutic engagement, not as treatment itself (Section 7).

**Principle 11: Treatment does not need to be voluntary to be effective.** Sanctions or enticements from family, employment settings, or the criminal justice system can significantly increase treatment entry, retention, and ultimate success. HOR's Outpatient Engagement Pathway uses Motivational Interviewing to support ambivalent or family-referred clients towards readiness, consistent with Prochaska and DiClemente's Transtheoretical Model research demonstrating that therapeutic engagement can facilitate movement from pre-contemplation to action stages (Section 4.5).

**Principle 12: Drug use during treatment must be monitored continuously.** Lapses during treatment do occur, and objective monitoring (e.g., urinalysis) can help the client withstand urges to use drugs. HOR conducts monthly urine drug screening (UDS) for all residents, with additional testing triggered by clinical suspicion or pass-related risk (Section 5.1).

**Principle 13: Treatment programs should test patients for the presence of HIV/AIDS, hepatitis B and C, tuberculosis, and other infectious diseases, as well as provide targeted risk-reduction counselling.** HOR's mandatory pre-admission medical panel includes HIV, Hepatitis A and B serology, chest X-ray for TB screening, and full blood count, with linkage to antiretroviral therapy (ART) services for HIV-positive clients (Section 4.8).

*Source: NIDA, Principles of Drug Addiction Treatment: A Research-Based Guide, Third Edition. National Institutes of Health, Publication No. 12-4180.*

### 2.2 WHO Mental Health Gap Action Programme (mhGAP), 2023 Update

The World Health Organization's mhGAP intervention guide is the primary international framework for scaling evidence-based mental health and substance use interventions in low- and middle-income countries. The 2023 update includes 30 updated recommendations and 18 new recommendations across mental, neurological, and substance use (MNS) conditions.

Key mhGAP principles operationalised at HOR include: (a) psychosocial interventions as first-line treatment for substance use disorders, consistent with HOR's emphasis on CBT, MI, and group therapy before pharmacological escalation; (b) integration of mental health screening instruments (PHQ-9, GAD-7) into routine substance abuse treatment assessment; (c) validation of non-specialist health worker delivery of evidence-based interventions under clinical supervision, which supports HOR's model of trained counsellors and house masters delivering structured psychosocial interventions under the oversight of the Program Director (Medical Doctor) and Clinical Lead; and (d) the inclusion of digitally delivered interventions across treatment modules, which HOR is incorporating through its digital client management platform for progress tracking, assessment scoring, and aftercare coordination.

The mhGAP framework is particularly relevant to HOR's context because it was designed specifically for resource-constrained settings where specialist psychiatric and addiction medicine capacity is limited. Its evidence base validates the feasibility and effectiveness of structured residential treatment programmes led by trained non-specialists with physician oversight.

*Source: WHO mhGAP Guideline for Mental, Neurological and Substance Use Disorders, 2023 Update. World Health Organization, Geneva.*

### 2.3 ASAM Criteria, Fourth Edition (2024)

The American Society of Addiction Medicine (ASAM) Criteria is the most widely used and comprehensive set of guidelines for patient placement, continued stay, and transfer/discharge of clients with addiction and co-occurring conditions. The Fourth Edition, published in 2024, provides what ASAM describes as its clearest withdrawal management guidance to date.

HOR operates at **ASAM Level 3.5: Clinically Managed High-Intensity Residential Services**, characterised by 24-hour care with trained counsellors, direct physician involvement for medical management, and a structured therapeutic community milieu.

The ASAM six-dimensional assessment framework governs HOR's admission and treatment matching decisions:

| Dimension | Domain | HOR Application |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|
| 1 | Acute Intoxication / Withdrawal Potential | CIWA-Ar and COWS scoring, detox protocol activation |
| 2 | Biomedical Conditions and Complications | Pre-admission medical panel, physician evaluation |
| 3 | Emotional, Behavioral, or Cognitive Conditions | PHQ-9, GAD-7, C-SSRS, psychosocial assessment |
| 4 | Readiness to Change | URICA staging, Motivational Interviewing assessment |
| 5 | Relapse, Continued Use, or Continued Problem Potential | Addiction Severity Index, substance history, triggers |
| 6 | Recovery / Living Environment | Family assessment (Pathway A), VNA (Pathway B), housing |

The ASAM dimensional framework ensures that admission decisions are clinically systematic rather than subjective, and that treatment intensity is matched to assessed severity across all six domains.

*Source: ASAM Criteria, Fourth Edition. American Society of Addiction Medicine, 2024.*

### 2.4 Therapeutic Community Model (De Leon)

The Therapeutic Community (TC) model, developed and researched extensively by Dr. George De Leon at Daytop Village in New York, provides the residential community framework for HOR's programme design. De Leon's research identifies the TC as operating on four interconnected views: the view of the substance use disorder, the view of the individual, the view of the recovery process, and the view of healthy living. The central principle is "community as method" -- the therapeutic community itself is the primary instrument of change, through structured daily routines, peer accountability, hierarchical work responsibilities, and collective governance.

The empirical evidence for the TC model is substantial. De Leon's longitudinal research at Daytop Village demonstrated that 88% of clients who completed two years in a therapeutic community were employed and crime-free at follow-up. More critically for HOR's 12-week programme design, TC outcome research consistently demonstrates that residents who remain in treatment for 90 or more days show outcomes approximately 9 times better than those who depart early (De Leon, 2000). This finding directly corroborates NIDA Principle 5 and provides the primary justification for HOR's non-negotiable 12-week minimum programme duration.

HOR implements TC principles through: structured daily schedules from 5:30 AM to 11:00 PM; morning and evening community meetings with peer accountability; hierarchical work roles and responsibilities within the facility; community governance through the behavioural management system; peer recovery circles; and graduated autonomy as clients progress through the four treatment phases.

*Source: De Leon, G. (2000). The Therapeutic Community: Theory, Model, and Method. Springer Publishing. De Leon, G. "The Therapeutic Community: A Research Summary," PMC, 2020. Daytop Village Outcomes Research Archives.*

### 2.5 Cognitive Behavioural Therapy: Systematic Review Evidence (2023)

A 2023 systematic review published in the journal Substance Abuse and Rehabilitation (PMC10572095) evaluated the cumulative evidence for CBT as a treatment for substance use disorders and issued a "strong recommendation" for CBT as an empirically supported treatment. The review synthesised findings across multiple meta-analyses and found:

- Effect sizes for CBT in SUD treatment range from small to large, depending on the substance involved. CBT demonstrates its strongest effects for cannabis and nicotine use disorders, with meaningful but smaller effects for opioid and alcohol use disorders.
- Meta-analytic data show a sharp decline in relapse rates within the first three months following CBT initiation, with stabilisation of gains after six months. This evidence identifies a "critical window for skill consolidation within three months" of treatment -- a finding that directly justifies HOR's 12-week programme as the minimum duration needed to complete the initial skill-building cycle.
- CBT's mechanism of action in SUD -- identifying and restructuring maladaptive cognitions, developing refusal skills, building coping strategy repertoires, and enhancing self-efficacy -- aligns with the broader TC model's emphasis on cognitive and behavioural change within a structured environment.

HOR delivers CBT through weekly individual sessions (60 minutes), thrice-weekly group sessions (90 minutes), and integrated relapse prevention planning (PRPP development in Phases 3-4).

*Source: Magill, M. et al. (2023). "Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Substance Use Disorders: A Systematic Review." Substance Abuse and Rehabilitation, PMC10572095.*

### 2.6 Motivational Interviewing and the Transtheoretical Model

Motivational Interviewing (MI), developed by Miller and Rollnick, is grounded in over 30 years of clinical research demonstrating its effectiveness for engaging ambivalent and resistant clients in substance use treatment. MI is theoretically anchored in the Transtheoretical Model of Change developed by Prochaska and DiClemente (1982), which identifies five stages through which individuals progress in behaviour change:

1. **Precontemplation** -- No intention to change; unaware of or minimising the problem
2. **Contemplation** -- Awareness of the problem; considering change but ambivalent
3. **Preparation** -- Intending to take action; making small behavioural steps
4. **Action** -- Active modification of behaviour, experiences, and environment
5. **Maintenance** -- Sustaining gains; preventing relapse

Research demonstrates that the Precontemplation subscale alone is a significant predictor of treatment dropout, making stage-of-change assessment essential at admission. MI facilitates movement through these stages by resolving ambivalence through empathic, non-confrontational dialogue. The URICA (University of Rhode Island Change Assessment) instrument, administered at HOR admission and bi-weekly thereafter, provides standardised measurement of a client's stage of change.

HOR applies MI throughout the treatment continuum: in pre-admission screening to assess readiness; in the Outpatient Engagement Pathway to build motivation in pre-contemplative clients; in early treatment phases to maintain engagement; and whenever resistance or ambivalence emerges during the programme.

*Source: Miller, W.R. & Rollnick, S. (2013). Motivational Interviewing: Helping People Change, Third Edition. Guilford Press. Prochaska, J.O. & DiClemente, C.C. (1982). "Transtheoretical Therapy: Toward a More Integrative Model of Change." Psychotherapy: Theory, Research & Practice, 19(3), 276-288.*

### 2.7 Cochrane Reviews: Withdrawal Management Evidence

HOR's detoxification protocols are grounded in Cochrane Collaboration systematic reviews, the gold standard for evidence-based clinical practice:

**Alcohol Withdrawal -- Symptom-Triggered Benzodiazepine Dosing:** Cochrane and Oxford Academic evidence demonstrates that symptom-triggered benzodiazepine dosing (medication administered only when CIWA-Ar scores exceed threshold) is superior to fixed-schedule dosing. In controlled trials, symptom-triggered protocols resulted in significantly lower mean benzodiazepine doses (9.5 mg diazepam equivalent versus 19.9 mg under fixed schedules) and dramatically shorter treatment durations (mean 47.8 hours versus 146 hours). This evidence supports HOR's adoption of CIWA-Ar-guided symptom-triggered dosing as the standard alcohol detoxification protocol.

**Opioid Withdrawal -- Clonidine (Alpha-2 Adrenergic Agonist):** Cochrane systematic review confirms that clonidine is more effective than placebo for managing opioid withdrawal symptoms, with particular importance in LMIC contexts where opioid agonist therapies (methadone, buprenorphine) may be unavailable or impractical. Clonidine addresses autonomic hyperactivity (sweating, tachycardia, hypertension, anxiety) while adjunctive medications -- loperamide for gastrointestinal symptoms, NSAIDs for musculoskeletal pain, promethazine for nausea -- provide comprehensive symptom management. This pharmacological approach forms the basis of HOR's opioid detoxification protocol.

*Sources: Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews: "Benzodiazepines for Alcohol Withdrawal" (multiple updates). Oxford Academic: Symptom-triggered versus fixed-schedule benzodiazepine dosing trials. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews: "Alpha-2 Adrenergic Agonists for the Management of Opioid Withdrawal."*

### 2.8 Trauma-Informed Care Research

The integration of trauma-informed care (TIC) into residential SUD treatment is supported by a growing body of evidence documenting the high prevalence of trauma histories among individuals with substance use disorders and the impact of unaddressed trauma on treatment outcomes.

A 2024 study published in the Journal of Substance Use & Addiction Treatment evaluated trauma-informed residential treatment and found that "clients had significant reductions in substance involvement, depression, anxiety, and PTSD symptoms" when trauma-informed principles were systematically integrated into the treatment milieu. This finding directly supports HOR's commitment to trauma-informed practice across all staff interactions and programme components.

Research on Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs) demonstrates that ACE scores are disproportionately elevated in SUD populations. Individuals with four or more ACEs are 7 times more likely to develop alcohol use disorder and 4.5 times more likely to develop depression compared to those with zero ACEs (Felitti et al., 1998; Centers for Disease Control and Prevention ACE Study). History of trauma is also associated with poorer SUD treatment retention, making trauma screening and responsive practice essential for reducing dropout.

The four core areas of TIC implementation are: (1) universal trauma screening at admission; (2) comprehensive staff training in trauma-aware practice; (3) environmental modifications to promote physical and psychological safety; and (4) integration of trauma-focused therapeutic modalities (e.g., trauma-focused CBT) for clients with identified trauma histories.

*Sources: Journal of Substance Use & Addiction Treatment (2024): Trauma-informed residential treatment outcomes. Felitti, V.J. et al. (1998). "Relationship of Childhood Abuse and Household Dysfunction to Many of the Leading Causes of Death in Adults," American Journal of Preventive Medicine, 14(4), 245-258. SAMHSA (2014). Concept of Trauma and Guidance for a Trauma-Informed Approach.*

### 2.9 Spirituality and Recovery: Research Evidence

The integration of spirituality into addiction recovery is supported by a substantial evidence base that extends well beyond anecdotal or faith-tradition claims:

**Prevalence and Client Demand:** Research indicates that 73% of US addiction treatment programmes include a spirituality-based element, and surveys of treatment clients show that 84% desire greater emphasis on spirituality in their recovery programming. This demand-side evidence supports the provision of spiritual formation as a core programme component rather than an optional add-on.

**Neurobiological Evidence:** Neuroimaging studies demonstrate that practices of prayer and meditation strengthen neural circuits involved in attention regulation, emotional processing, and impulse control -- precisely the cognitive domains impaired by chronic substance use and critical for sustained recovery. These findings provide a neurobiological mechanism through which spiritual practices contribute to recovery outcomes.

**Faith-Based Programme Outcomes:** Adult & Teen Challenge, the world's largest faith-based residential recovery network (215+ centres), has been the subject of multiple outcome studies. Research shows that 74% of programme graduates report no substance use at six months post-completion, and 87.5% of former marijuana users were abstinent seven years after programme completion. Studies identify four elements contributing to recovery in faith-based settings: programme structure, relationships with others, experiences of God, and individual initiative.

**Economic Impact:** Faith-based volunteer support in addiction recovery contributes an estimated $316.6 billion in annual savings to the US economy through reduced healthcare utilisation, criminal justice costs, and productivity losses.

**Theoretical Framework:** Brown et al. (2024) published the Spirituality in Recovery Framework, providing an actionable, research-informed model for integrating spiritual transformation into clinical treatment. This framework guides HOR's approach to spiritual formation as a structured, measurable component of the recovery process rather than an undefined spiritual aspiration.

*Sources: Brown, A.E. et al. (2024). "Spirituality in Recovery Framework." Journal of Religion and Health. Teen Challenge Research: Wilder Research Study (2009); Dr. Catherine Hess Study; ACADC Research Review. Neuroimaging: Newberg, A.B. (2014). "The Neuroscientific Study of Spiritual Practices," Frontiers in Psychology.*

### 2.10 Continuing Care (Aftercare) Research

Research on continuing care following residential SUD treatment demonstrates that post-discharge support is not optional -- it is essential for sustained recovery outcomes.

McKay and colleagues' research on continuing care demonstrates that **12 or more months of structured aftercare is associated with significantly better long-term abstinence rates** compared to shorter or no aftercare (Journal of Substance Abuse Treatment). Furthermore, **rapid initiation of aftercare services within 14 days of residential discharge** significantly reduces relapse risk during the high-vulnerability transition period. The research consensus is that a **combination of services post-discharge** (individual check-ins, group support, family engagement, vocational support) represents best practice.

Mindfulness-Based Relapse Prevention (MBRP), developed by Bowen et al., has demonstrated **lower relapse rates at 12 months compared to treatment-as-usual** in randomised controlled trials. MBRP integrates mindfulness meditation practices with cognitive-behavioural relapse prevention strategies, providing clients with present-moment awareness skills that enhance their ability to recognise and manage cravings and high-risk situations.

These findings inform HOR's 24-month aftercare protocol with weekly contact in the first three months, structured outcome assessments at 3, 6, 12, and 24 months, and relapse response protocols activated within 48 hours of any identified lapse.

*Sources: McKay, J.R. (2009). "Continuing Care Research: What We've Learned and Where We're Going," Journal of Substance Abuse Treatment, 36(2), 131-145. Bowen, S. et al. (2014). "Relative Efficacy of Mindfulness-Based Relapse Prevention, Standard Relapse Prevention, and Treatment as Usual," JAMA Psychiatry, 71(5), 547-556.*

### 2.11 UNODC TREATNET Quality Standards

The United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) TREATNET programme, developed by an international consortium led by UCLA, provides quality standards specifically designed for drug dependence treatment in developing countries. Over 10,000 practitioners across 20+ countries have been trained using the TREATNET framework.

TREATNET standards address: comprehensive biopsychosocial assessment, individualised treatment planning, delivery of evidence-based interventions (CBT, MI, contingency management), structured community reintegration planning, and systematic aftercare follow-up. HOR's protocol is designed to meet or exceed TREATNET quality standards across all domains.

Of particular relevance to HOR is TREATNET's emphasis on culturally adapted implementation -- the recognition that evidence-based interventions must be delivered within local cultural frameworks to be effective. HOR's integration of Christian spiritual formation within an evidence-based clinical structure is consistent with TREATNET's principle that treatment programmes should incorporate culturally meaningful elements that enhance engagement and motivation.

*Source: UNODC TREATNET Training Package, Volumes I-III. United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime, Vienna. Available at unodc.org/treatment-and-care.*

## 4. ADMISSION PROTOCOL

### 4.1 Evidence Base for Admission Design

HOR's admission protocol integrates two primary research frameworks:

1. **ASAM Six-Dimensional Assessment (2024):** Every admission decision is informed by systematic evaluation across ASAM's six dimensions -- Acute Intoxication/Withdrawal Potential, Biomedical Conditions, Emotional/Behavioral/Cognitive Conditions, Readiness to Change, Relapse/Continued Use Potential, and Recovery/Living Environment. This ensures treatment placement is clinically appropriate and matched to individual severity.

2. **Prochaska & DiClemente's Transtheoretical Model (1982):** Client willingness is not assessed as a binary yes/no but as a position on the empirically validated stages-of-change continuum. Clients in the Precontemplation or early Contemplation stages are not rejected but are offered stage-appropriate services (Outpatient Engagement Pathway) consistent with NIDA Principle 11 (treatment does not need to be voluntary to be effective) and research showing that the Precontemplation subscale predicts treatment dropout.

### 4.2 The Willingness Criterion -- Primary Gateway

Admission to residential care requires demonstrated readiness to participate in recovery, assessed through validated instruments rather than subjective judgement:

- **URICA (University of Rhode Island Change Assessment):** Standardised measurement of stage of change. Clients scoring in the Action or Preparation stages are prioritised for immediate residential admission. Clients in Contemplation are admitted with enhanced motivational support. Clients in Precontemplation are enrolled in the Outpatient Engagement Pathway.
- **Motivational Interviewing-based dialogue:** Structured clinical interview using MI techniques (OARS: Open questions, Affirmations, Reflective listening, Summaries) to assess motivation and resolve ambivalence.
- **Clinical interview by Program Director (Medical Doctor):** Comprehensive medical and psychosocial evaluation.

**Unwilling clients are NOT turned away.** Consistent with NIDA Principle 11, they are enrolled in the Outpatient Engagement Pathway (Section 4.5).

### 5.2 CIWA-Ar Scoring Protocol (Alcohol Withdrawal)

The CIWA-Ar is the internationally validated standard for monitoring alcohol withdrawal severity, endorsed by the Cochrane Collaboration and ASAM Clinical Practice Guidelines for guiding pharmacological intervention.

**10 assessment items:** Nausea/vomiting, Tremor, Paroxysmal sweats, Anxiety, Agitation, Tactile disturbances, Auditory disturbances, Visual disturbances, Headache, Orientation/clouding of sensorium

| Score Range | Severity | Clinical Action | Evidence Basis |
|-------------|----------|-----------------|----------------|
| < 8 | Mild | Continue monitoring q4h; no medication indicated | Cochrane: scores <8 do not require pharmacological intervention |
| 8-15 | Moderate | Initiate symptom-triggered benzodiazepine protocol; monitor q2h | Cochrane: medication threshold at score >8; symptom-triggered dosing reduces mean dose (9.5mg vs 19.9mg) |
| 15-20 | Severe | Intensify symptom-triggered protocol; monitor q1h; seizure precautions | ASAM Clinical Practice Guideline: escalation at higher CIWA-Ar scores |
| > 20 | Very Severe / DT Risk | Intensive monitoring; consider transfer to higher level of care if not stabilising within 24 hours | ASAM: delirium tremens risk increases significantly above CIWA-Ar 20 |

### 5.3 COWS Scoring Protocol (Opioid Withdrawal)

The COWS is recommended by NIDA and ASAM for standardised monitoring of opioid withdrawal severity, guiding the timing and intensity of symptomatic management.

**11 assessment items:** Resting pulse rate, Sweating, Restlessness, Pupil size, Bone/joint aches, Runny nose/tearing, GI upset, Tremor, Yawning, Anxiety/irritability, Gooseflesh skin

| Score Range | Severity | Clinical Action | Evidence Basis |
|-------------|----------|-----------------|----------------|
| 0-4 | Minimal | Continue supportive care and hydration | NIDA/ASAM: subclinical withdrawal |
| 5-12 | Mild | Supportive care; comfort medications (loperamide, NSAIDs) | Cochrane: adjunctive symptom management |
| 13-24 | Moderate | Clonidine protocol initiated; close monitoring | Cochrane: clonidine more effective than placebo for autonomic symptoms |
| 25-36 | Moderately Severe | Active pharmacological management; clonidine + full adjunctive protocol | Cochrane: alpha-2 agonist efficacy confirmed |
| > 36 | Severe | Intensive management; consider transfer if not stabilising | ASAM Level 4 criteria may be indicated |

### 5.4 C-SSRS Risk Assessment Protocol

The Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale was developed at Columbia University and validated through FDA-mandated clinical trials involving over 15,000 participants. It has been adopted as the standard suicide risk screening instrument by the US military, multiple national health systems, and the World Health Organization.

**6 screening questions (all Yes/No):**

1. Wish to be dead
2. Suicidal thoughts (non-specific)
3. Suicidal thoughts with method (without plan)
4. Suicidal intent (without specific plan)
5. Suicidal intent with plan
6. Suicidal behaviour (with timeframe: lifetime / past 3 months / past month / since last visit)

**Additional safety screening:**
- Active psychosis indicators (select from standard list)
- Homicidal ideation (Yes/No)
- Self-harm history (None / Historical / Past 3 months / Current)

| Risk Level | Criteria | Required Action |
|------------|----------|-----------------|
| **Low** | Q1-2 only, no other flags | Continue routine weekly monitoring |
| **Moderate** | Q3-4, or past self-harm | Increase observation frequency; notify Clinical Lead; document in risk register |
| **High** | Q5, or Q3-4 with self-harm history | Immediate 1-to-1 supervision; notify Program Director; contact family/PFSP |
| **Critical** | Q6 (recent), or active psychosis/homicidal ideation | Emergency protocol: refer to Yaba Psychiatric Hospital or nearest A&E |

## 6. TREATMENT MODEL: 4-PHASE / 12-WEEK PROGRAMME

### 6.1 Evidence Base for Programme Duration and Structure

HOR's 12-week (84-day) minimum programme duration is justified by the convergence of three independent evidence streams:

1. **NIDA Principle 5:** "Research indicates that most addicted individuals need at least three months in treatment to significantly reduce or stop their drug use and that the best outcomes occur with longer durations of treatment."

2. **De Leon's Therapeutic Community Research:** TC outcome data demonstrate that residents who remain in treatment for 90 or more days show outcomes approximately 9 times better than those who depart early. The 90-day threshold represents the minimum duration for meaningful internalisation of community norms, development of peer accountability, and acquisition of sustained behavioural change (De Leon, 2000).

3. **CBT Meta-Analytic Evidence (2023):** The systematic review (PMC10572095) identifies a "critical window for skill consolidation within three months" of CBT initiation. Meta-analytic data show a sharp decline in relapse rates within the first three months, with stabilisation of treatment gains after six months. The 12-week programme captures this critical consolidation period.

The four-phase structure is grounded in TC staged treatment research, which demonstrates that recovery progresses through sequential stages of stabilisation, skill building, deepening, and reintegration -- each requiring distinct therapeutic emphases and graduated levels of client autonomy.

### 6.2 Phase Overview

| Phase | Weeks | Focus | Key Milestones | Evidence Basis |
|-------|-------|-------|----------------|----------------|
| **Phase 1: Medical Stabilisation** | 1-2 | Detoxification, vitals monitoring, nutritional recovery, orientation, baseline assessments | Medical detox complete, all baseline assessments complete, treatment plan initiated | NIDA Principle 10 (detox = first stage only); ASAM withdrawal management |
| **Phase 2: Therapeutic Foundation** | 3-6 | CBT initiation, relapse prevention, psychoeducation, individual counselling, spiritual formation begins, life skills begin | Week 4 MDT treatment plan review, triggers identified, coping skills developing | CBT skill acquisition phase (PMC10572095); Transtheoretical Model Action stage |
| **Phase 3: Deepening & Skills Building** | 7-10 | Advanced therapy, trauma processing, family systems, vocational assessment, PRPP development | Week 8 MDT review, PRPP drafted, vocational pathway identified, family therapy sessions | CBT skill consolidation (critical window); TC deepening phase (De Leon) |
| **Phase 4: Reintegration & Graduation** | 11-12 | Pre-discharge assessment, family/community meeting, aftercare plan, graduation ceremony, Alumni Programme enrolment | All 6 graduation criteria met, PRPP finalised, church placement confirmed, aftercare plan signed | TC reintegration phase; McKay continuing care evidence |

## 7. DETOXIFICATION PROTOCOL

### 7.1 Scope and Evidence Base

Medical detoxification is conducted under physician supervision with 24/7 nursing monitoring during Weeks 1-2. Consistent with NIDA Principle 10, detoxification is positioned as the medically necessary first stage of treatment that by itself does little to change long-term drug use. The goal is safe, medically managed withdrawal with symptom relief -- not treatment completion.

HOR's detoxification protocols are grounded in the ASAM Clinical Practice Guideline for Alcohol Withdrawal Management (2024), Cochrane systematic reviews on benzodiazepine management and alpha-2 adrenergic agonist efficacy, and WHO mhGAP recommendations for withdrawal management in LMIC settings.

### 7.2 Alcohol Detoxification Protocol

**Evidence Base:** The ASAM Clinical Practice Guideline (2024) and Cochrane systematic reviews provide the strongest available evidence for alcohol withdrawal management. Symptom-triggered benzodiazepine dosing, guided by CIWA-Ar scores, has been demonstrated in controlled trials to produce:
- Significantly lower mean benzodiazepine doses (9.5 mg diazepam equivalent vs 19.9 mg under fixed-schedule protocols)
- Dramatically shorter treatment durations (mean 47.8 hours vs 146 hours)
- Equivalent or superior safety outcomes compared to fixed-schedule dosing

**Protocol:**

| Element | Standard | Evidence Basis |
|---------|----------|----------------|
| Assessment | CIWA-Ar scoring at admission, then q4h (q2h if score >15, q1h if >20) | Cochrane: validated CIWA-Ar thresholds |
| Medication | Symptom-triggered benzodiazepine protocol (Chlordiazepoxide or Diazepam); medication initiated when CIWA-Ar > 8 | Cochrane: symptom-triggered superior to fixed-schedule; 9.5mg vs 19.9mg mean dose |
| Nutritional Support | Thiamine 100mg IM/IV x 3-5 days, then oral; Multivitamin; Hydration monitoring | ASAM CPG: thiamine to prevent Wernicke's encephalopathy; WHO nutritional guidelines |
| Seizure Precautions | Seizure precautions for all clients with CIWA-Ar > 15 or history of withdrawal seizures | ASAM CPG: 3-5% of untreated alcohol withdrawal progresses to seizures |
| Monitoring | Vital signs q4h minimum; escalate per CIWA-Ar scoring schedule | ASAM withdrawal management guidance (2024) |
| Duration | Typically 5-7 days; extended monitoring as clinically indicated | Cochrane: symptom-triggered protocol mean duration 47.8 hours |
| Completion Criteria | CIWA-Ar < 8 for 24 consecutive hours; vital signs stable; no seizure risk indicators | ASAM completion criteria |

### 7.3 Opioid Detoxification Protocol

**Evidence Base:** The Cochrane systematic review on alpha-2 adrenergic agonists confirms that clonidine is more effective than placebo for managing opioid withdrawal symptoms. This evidence is particularly relevant for LMIC contexts such as Nigeria where opioid agonist therapies (methadone, buprenorphine) may be unavailable, tightly regulated, or impractical for residential settings. Clonidine addresses the autonomic hyperactivity (sympathetic nervous system overdrive) that characterises opioid withdrawal, while adjunctive medications manage specific symptom clusters.

**Protocol:**

| Element | Standard | Evidence Basis |
|---------|----------|----------------|
| Assessment | COWS scoring at admission, then per clinical schedule | NIDA/ASAM: COWS as standard opioid withdrawal measure |
| Primary Medication | Clonidine (alpha-2 adrenergic agonist) for autonomic symptoms: sweating, tachycardia, hypertension, anxiety | Cochrane: clonidine more effective than placebo; important in LMIC where agonist therapy unavailable |
| GI Symptom Management | Loperamide for diarrhoea and abdominal cramping | Cochrane adjunctive protocol |
| Pain Management | NSAIDs (Ibuprofen) for musculoskeletal pain, bone/joint aches | Cochrane adjunctive protocol; non-opioid analgesic |
| Antiemetic | Promethazine for nausea and vomiting | Cochrane adjunctive protocol |
| Monitoring | Vital signs (blood pressure critical with clonidine); hydration; psychological support | Clonidine hypotension risk monitoring |
| Duration | Typically 7-14 days depending on substance and duration of use | ASAM withdrawal timeline guidance |
| Completion Criteria | COWS < 5 for 24 consecutive hours; vital signs stable; adequate oral intake | ASAM completion criteria |

**Important:** No opioid-containing medications are permitted in the facility under any circumstances. No opioid agonist therapy (methadone, buprenorphine) is administered at HOR.

### 7.4 Cannabis / Stimulant Withdrawal Protocol

**Evidence Base:** Unlike alcohol and opioid withdrawal, cannabis and stimulant withdrawal syndromes do not have validated pharmacological protocols. However, research recognises cannabis withdrawal syndrome (DSM-5) and stimulant withdrawal as clinically significant conditions requiring symptomatic management.

| Element | Standard |
|---------|----------|
| Assessment | Clinical observation; mood, sleep, appetite, agitation monitoring |
| Monitoring | Daily clinical review; PHQ-9 if depressive symptoms emerge |
| Support | Sleep hygiene education; anxiolytics if indicated; nutritional support; graduated physical activity |
| Duration | Variable; typically 3-7 days for acute symptoms |

### 7.5 General Medication Management During Detox

- All medications surrendered to nursing staff on admission
- All medications administered exclusively by licensed nursing staff
- Documented in Medication Administration Record (MAR) at each round
- Medication storage: locked cabinet, controlled access, stock reconciliation weekly
- No resident retains any medication (exceptions: nitroglycerin, insulin, seizure medication, asthma medication -- doctor-approved only)

## 8. THERAPEUTIC INTERVENTIONS

### 8.1 Treatment Plan Structure (Columbia Model)

Each client's treatment plan follows a structured Problem-Goal-Intervention model, consistent with NIDA Principles 1 (individualised), 3 (multiple needs), and 4 (continually assessed and modified):

**Step 1: Problem Identification** (DSM-5 aligned, ASAM 6-dimensional)
- Substance Use Disorder (ASAM Dimension 1, 5)
- Withdrawal Management (ASAM Dimension 1)
- Co-occurring Mental Health (ASAM Dimension 3; NIDA Principle 9)
- Relapse Risk (ASAM Dimension 5)
- Trauma History (ACE screening; 2024 TIC evidence)
- Family/Relationship Dysfunction (ASAM Dimension 6)
- Housing Instability (ASAM Dimension 6)
- Employment/Vocational Deficit (TC model; UNODC TREATNET)
- Spiritual Disconnection (Brown et al., 2024)
- Legal Complications

**Step 2: SMART Goals** (Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, Time-bound)
- 3-7 goals per identified problem
- Goals co-authored with client (NIDA Principle 1: individualised)
- Reviewed at MDT (Week 4, Week 8, Pre-discharge) per NIDA Principle 4

**Step 3: Interventions** (linked to goals, each evidence-based)
- Individual CBT (PMC10572095: "strong recommendation")
- Group CBT (meta-analytic support for group delivery)
- Motivational Interviewing (Miller & Rollnick, 2013)
- Psychoeducation (NIDA Principle 3)
- Spiritual Formation (Brown et al., 2024)
- Vocational Training (De Leon TC model)
- Family Therapy (family SUD evidence base)
- Pastoral Counselling (spirituality in recovery evidence)
- Trauma-focused interventions where indicated (2024 TIC study)

**Step 4: Progress Tracking**
- Goal status: Not Started / In Progress / Completed / Deferred / Modified
- Treatment plan updated at each MDT review (NIDA Principle 4, 8)
- Client participates in all reviews (person-centred care)

### 8.2 Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)

**Evidence Base:** The 2023 systematic review (PMC10572095) issues a "strong recommendation" for CBT as an empirically supported treatment for substance use disorders. Meta-analytic effect sizes range from small to large depending on the substance, with the strongest effects observed for cannabis and nicotine use disorders. The review identifies the mechanism through which CBT operates in SUD: identifying maladaptive cognitions that maintain substance use, restructuring these thought patterns, developing behavioural coping strategies, and building self-efficacy for abstinence.

**Delivery at HOR:**

| Modality | Format | Evidence Basis |
|----------|--------|----------------|
| Individual CBT | 60-minute sessions, weekly minimum per client | PMC10572095: individual CBT recommended for SUD |
| Group CBT | 90-minute sessions, 3x weekly, max 20 participants | Group delivery meta-analysis: comparable efficacy with added peer modelling |

**Core CBT Components at HOR:**
- Thought records and cognitive restructuring
- HALT model (Hungry, Angry, Lonely, Tired) -- empirically derived high-risk state identification
- Trigger mapping across five categories (People, Places, Feelings, Situations, Times)
- Coping strategy toolbox development
- Refusal skills training (behavioural rehearsal)
- PRPP (Personal Relapse Prevention Plan) development in Phases 3-4

### 8.3 Motivational Interviewing (MI)

**Evidence Base:** Over 30 years of clinical research support MI as effective for engaging ambivalent clients and facilitating movement through the stages of change (Miller & Rollnick, 2013; Prochaska & DiClemente, 1982). MI is the evidence-based intervention of choice for clients in the Precontemplation and Contemplation stages.

**Application at HOR:**
- Pre-admission screening (stage-of-change assessment)
- Outpatient Engagement Pathway (weekly MI sessions for pre-contemplative clients)
- Early treatment engagement (Phases 1-2)
- Whenever resistance, ambivalence, or motivational decline is observed
- Core MI techniques: OARS (Open questions, Affirmations, Reflective listening, Summaries)
- All clinical staff trained in MI principles and techniques

### 8.4 Personal Relapse Prevention Plan (PRPP)

**Evidence Base:** Relapse prevention is a cognitive-behavioural approach originally developed by Marlatt and Gordon (1985) and supported by meta-analytic evidence demonstrating its efficacy in reducing substance use post-treatment. The PRPP is the operationalised, personalised application of relapse prevention science.

Every client must develop a written PRPP as a graduation requirement:

## 9. TRAUMA-INFORMED CARE

### 9.1 Evidence Base and Rationale

This section establishes trauma-informed care (TIC) as a foundational principle of HOR's treatment approach, grounded in the following evidence:

**Prevalence of Trauma in SUD Populations:** Research on Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs) demonstrates that individuals with four or more ACEs are 7 times more likely to develop alcohol use disorder and 4.5 times more likely to develop depression (Felitti et al., 1998). The ACE Study, conducted by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention and Kaiser Permanente, established the dose-response relationship between childhood adversity and adult health outcomes, including substance use disorders. The prevalence of significant trauma histories in SUD treatment populations is consistently reported at 50-75% across studies.

**Treatment Outcomes:** A 2024 study in the Journal of Substance Use & Addiction Treatment found that when trauma-informed principles were systematically integrated into residential SUD treatment, "clients had significant reductions in substance involvement, depression, anxiety, and PTSD symptoms." This study provides direct evidence that TIC improves clinical outcomes in settings comparable to HOR.

**Treatment Retention:** History of trauma is associated with poorer SUD treatment retention. Trauma-informed approaches that create psychological safety, avoid re-traumatisation, and address trauma as a contributing factor to substance use improve retention rates -- directly supporting NIDA Principle 5 (adequate time in treatment is critical).

### 9.2 Four Pillars of TIC at HOR

Consistent with SAMHSA's Concept of Trauma and Guidance for a Trauma-Informed Approach (2014) and the 2024 residential TIC outcomes research:

**Pillar 1: Universal Trauma Screening**
- ACE (Adverse Childhood Experiences) questionnaire administered at admission for all clients
- Trauma history incorporated into ASAM Dimension 3 (Emotional/Behavioral/Cognitive) assessment
- Screening results inform treatment plan -- clients with significant trauma histories receive enhanced trauma-focused interventions
- Screening conducted by trained clinical staff in a safe, private setting

**Pillar 2: Comprehensive Staff Training**
- All staff (clinical and non-clinical) receive TIC orientation within 30 days of hire
- Training covers: understanding trauma and its impact on brain and behaviour; recognising trauma responses (fight, flight, freeze, fawn); avoiding re-traumatisation; responding with safety, trustworthiness, and empowerment
- Annual refresher training for all staff
- Clinical staff receive additional training in trauma-focused CBT techniques

**Pillar 3: Environmental Safety**
- Physical environment designed to promote safety: clear sight lines, adequate lighting, low-stimulus spaces available
- Predictable daily routine (Section 6.3) provides psychological safety through structure
- Staff interactions guided by principles of safety, trustworthiness, collaboration, empowerment, and cultural sensitivity
- No use of coercion, shaming, or punitive practices that could re-traumatise

**Pillar 4: Trauma-Focused Therapeutic Interventions**
- Trauma-focused CBT available for clients with identified trauma histories (Phase 3)
- Integration of trauma processing into individual counselling sessions
- Grounding techniques (5-4-3-2-1 sensory technique) taught in Psychoeducation groups
- Referral to specialist trauma therapy for clients with complex PTSD presentations exceeding HOR scope
- Spiritual formation as a trauma-healing modality: forgiveness work, identity reconstruction, meaning-making

### 9.3 Integration with Clinical Protocols

TIC principles are not a separate programme but are woven throughout all HOR operations:

| Domain | TIC Integration |
|--------|-----------------|
| Admission | Trauma screening; compassionate intake process; explanation of all procedures before they occur |
| Assessment | ACE questionnaire; trauma history in clinical formulation; awareness that assessment can trigger trauma responses |
| Group Therapy | Ground rules include emotional safety; no pressure to disclose trauma in group settings; opt-out provisions |
| Individual Therapy | Trauma processing available in Phase 3 when therapeutic alliance is established and client is stabilised |
| Behavioural Management | Discipline understood through trauma lens; behaviour as communication; no shaming |
| Spiritual Formation | Trauma-sensitive pastoral care; recognition that some clients have religious trauma |
| Discharge | Trauma history incorporated into PRPP trigger identification; aftercare includes trauma support |

## 15. BEHAVIOURAL MANAGEMENT PROTOCOL

### 15.1 Evidence Base

The behavioural management system at HOR is grounded in the Therapeutic Community model's principle of community governance as a therapeutic instrument (De Leon, 2000). In the TC framework, behavioural expectations are not arbitrary rules but therapeutic interventions that teach self-governance, delayed gratification, and accountability -- core competencies impaired by addiction and essential for sustained recovery.

De Leon's research demonstrates that the TC's structured behavioural system, including clear expectations, graduated consequences, and community accountability, contributes directly to the internalisation of prosocial norms that differentiate successful long-term recovery from mere short-term abstinence.

The system is also informed by trauma-informed care principles (Section 9): all behavioural responses are designed to be non-shaming, non-coercive, and oriented towards growth rather than punishment.

> *"No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it."* -- Hebrews 12:11

### 15.2 Three-Tier System

- Examples: Poor hygiene, dormitory cleanliness, lateness, food in rooms, phone abuse, laziness on duties

- Applied for: Disengagement pattern, spiritual stagnation, consistent failure to progress

- Measures: Special work crew, graduation date on hold, loss of all pass/phone, all free time redirected to prayer/Bible study/reflection, weekly evaluation meetings

## 17. CONTINUING CARE & ALUMNI PROTOCOL

### 17.1 Evidence Base

The continuing care research evidence is unequivocal: post-discharge support is essential for sustained recovery outcomes.

**Duration:** McKay's systematic review (Journal of Substance Abuse Treatment, 2009) demonstrates that **12 or more months of structured aftercare is associated with significantly better long-term abstinence rates** compared to shorter or no aftercare. HOR's 24-month monitoring protocol exceeds this evidence-based minimum.

**Rapid Initiation:** Research demonstrates that **initiating aftercare services within 14 days of residential discharge** significantly reduces relapse risk during the high-vulnerability transition period. HOR mandates first aftercare contact within 7 days of discharge, exceeding the evidence-based threshold.

**Combination of Services:** The research consensus is that a **combination of post-discharge services** (telephone monitoring, individual check-ins, group support, family engagement, vocational support) produces better outcomes than any single service modality. HOR's aftercare protocol integrates multiple service streams.

**MBRP Evidence:** Mindfulness-Based Relapse Prevention (MBRP; Bowen et al., 2014) has demonstrated **lower relapse rates at 12 months compared to treatment-as-usual** in randomised controlled trials published in JAMA Psychiatry. MBRP integrates mindfulness meditation with cognitive-behavioural relapse prevention, and its principles are incorporated into HOR's relaxation and stress management programming.

### 17.4 Relapse Response Protocol

3. Outreach counselling offered (MI-based re-engagement)
4. Residential re-admission offered (if willing; Section 16.5)
5. Harm reduction information provided
6. Family/support network engaged (with consent)

## 20. SUBSTANCE-SPECIFIC CLINICAL PATHWAYS

### 20.1 Rationale

The base 4-phase / 12-week programme (Section 6) provides the universal treatment structure for all clients. However, research evidence demonstrates that substance-specific differences in pharmacology, withdrawal presentation, relapse patterns, cognitive impairment profiles, and social contexts require differentiated clinical approaches within the shared programme framework.

This section establishes five substance-specific clinical pathways. Each pathway modifies the base protocol in targeted areas while maintaining the universal programme structure, daily schedule, spiritual formation, and community elements.

**Pathway activation:** The treating physician assigns the primary substance pathway at admission based on the ASI, ASSIST, UDS results, and clinical interview. Polysubstance clients are assigned based on the substance presenting the greatest clinical severity, with secondary pathway elements integrated into the treatment plan.

### 20.2 Alcohol Use Disorder (AUD) Pathway

**Prevalence context:** Alcohol is the most commonly reported substance of use among Nigerian treatment populations. AUD clients typically present with longer use histories, more severe medical comorbidities, and greater family system disruption than other substance groups.

#### Phase 1 Modifications (Weeks 1-2): Detoxification

| Element | AUD-Specific Protocol | Evidence Basis |
|---------|----------------------|----------------|
| Primary instrument | CIWA-Ar scored at admission, then q4h (q2h if >15, q1h if >20) | Cochrane: CIWA-Ar validated thresholds |
| Pharmacological management | Symptom-triggered benzodiazepine protocol (Chlordiazepoxide or Diazepam); initiated when CIWA-Ar > 8 | Cochrane: symptom-triggered superior to fixed-schedule |
| Nutritional priority | Thiamine 100mg IM/IV x 3-5 days then oral; B-complex; folate; hydration monitoring | ASAM CPG: Wernicke's encephalopathy prevention |
| Seizure risk | Seizure precautions for all clients with CIWA-Ar > 15 or history of withdrawal seizures; padded side rails, suction equipment accessible | ASAM: 3-5% of untreated alcohol withdrawal progresses to seizures |
| Hepatic monitoring | LFTs at admission, repeat at Week 4 and discharge; adjust all hepatically metabolised medications | ASAM Dimension 2 |
| Completion criteria | CIWA-Ar < 8 for 24 consecutive hours; vital signs stable; no seizure risk indicators | ASAM completion criteria |
| Typical duration | 5-7 days; extended monitoring as clinically indicated | Cochrane: symptom-triggered mean 47.8 hours |

#### Phase 2 Modifications (Weeks 3-6): Therapeutic Foundation

| Element | AUD-Specific Focus |
|---------|-------------------|
| CBT emphasis | Alcohol-specific cognitive distortions: "I can control my drinking," "One drink won't hurt," "I need alcohol to socialise." Drink refusal skills training with behavioural rehearsal |
| Trigger mapping | AUD-specific triggers: social drinking culture, family celebrations, business entertaining, payday patterns, evening routines, specific brands/venues |
| Psychoeducation | Alcohol and the brain: neuroadaptation, kindling effect (each withdrawal episode more severe), liver disease progression, alcohol-related cognitive impairment, foetal alcohol spectrum |
| Medical monitoring | Weekly LFT trend review; nutritional status; GI symptom management; sleep disturbance (common in early AUD recovery -- typically resolves by Week 6-8) |
| Family education | Enabling behaviours specific to alcohol: covering for drunkenness, removing consequences, social normalisation of drinking |

#### Phase 3-4 Modifications (Weeks 7-12): Deepening & Reintegration

| Element | AUD-Specific Focus |
|---------|-------------------|
| PRPP development | Alcohol-specific relapse prevention: navigating social drinking environments, managing family/cultural pressure to drink, alternative socialising strategies, wedding/celebration plans |
| Vocational | Assess whether employment involves alcohol exposure (hospitality, entertainment); develop workplace sobriety plan if applicable |
| Aftercare | AA/recovery fellowship placement; identify alcohol-free social networks; family ongoing psychoeducation |
| Outcome measures | AUDIT (admission vs discharge); LFT trend; GGT as biological marker of abstinence |

### 20.3 Opioid Use Disorder (OUD) Pathway

**Prevalence context:** Opioid use in Nigeria includes tramadol, codeine-containing cough syrups, and increasingly heroin. Tramadol misuse is particularly prevalent among young males. OUD clients often present with significant pain complaints, higher rates of injection-related complications (heroin users), and strong physical dependence.

#### Phase 1 Modifications (Weeks 1-2): Detoxification

| Element | OUD-Specific Protocol | Evidence Basis |
|---------|----------------------|----------------|
| Primary instrument | COWS scored at admission, then per clinical schedule | NIDA/ASAM: COWS standard for opioid withdrawal |
| Pharmacological management | Clonidine (alpha-2 agonist) for autonomic symptoms; loperamide for GI symptoms; NSAIDs for musculoskeletal pain; promethazine for nausea | Cochrane: clonidine > placebo for opioid withdrawal |
| Pain assessment | Differentiate withdrawal-related pain from chronic pain conditions; establish non-opioid pain management plan | No opioid-containing medications permitted in facility |
| Infection screening | Enhanced screening for injection drug users: HIV, Hepatitis B/C, injection site infections, endocarditis risk | NIDA Principle 13; IDU-specific medical risks |
| Sleep management | Expect significant insomnia (7-14 days); sleep hygiene education; short-term non-benzodiazepine sedation if indicated | OUD withdrawal sleep disruption evidence |
| Completion criteria | COWS < 5 for 24 consecutive hours; vital signs stable; adequate oral intake | ASAM completion criteria |
| Typical duration | 7-14 days depending on opioid type, dose, and duration of use | ASAM withdrawal timeline; tramadol withdrawal can be prolonged |

#### Phase 2 Modifications (Weeks 3-6): Therapeutic Foundation

| Element | OUD-Specific Focus |
|---------|-------------------|
| CBT emphasis | Pain catastrophising and pain-related drug seeking; "I can't cope without it" cognitions; managing post-acute withdrawal syndrome (PAWS): mood instability, anhedonia, fatigue lasting weeks to months |
| Trigger mapping | OUD-specific triggers: physical pain, pharmacies, former dealers, codeine-containing OTC products, peer networks, emotional numbness |
| Psychoeducation | Opioid receptors and tolerance; overdose risk after abstinence (dramatically reduced tolerance); naloxone education; tramadol-specific seizure risk; codeine metabolism |
| Medical monitoring | PAWS symptom tracking; chronic pain management plan; mood monitoring (anhedonia common in early opioid recovery) |
| Spiritual formation | Identity beyond substance: many OUD clients have used opioids for emotional regulation since adolescence; identity reconstruction work critical |

#### Phase 3-4 Modifications (Weeks 7-12): Deepening & Reintegration

| Element | OUD-Specific Focus |
|---------|-------------------|
| PRPP development | Opioid-specific relapse prevention: pain management plan (non-opioid), pharmacy avoidance strategies, overdose risk education for family, naloxone access plan (where available) |
| Trauma processing | OUD populations show highest trauma comorbidity rates; prioritise trauma-focused CBT in Phase 3 |
| Aftercare | NA fellowship placement; pain management follow-up; if tramadol -- OTC medication vigilance plan; overdose prevention education for family |
| Outcome measures | DAST-10 (admission vs discharge); COWS resolution timeline; pain severity tracking |

### 20.4 Cannabis Use Disorder (CUD) Pathway

**Prevalence context:** Cannabis is the most widely used illicit substance in Nigeria. CUD clients often present younger, with less insight into cannabis as a problem substance, and frequently report cannabis as "natural" or "harmless." Cognitive impairment (memory, motivation, executive function) is often more pronounced than physical withdrawal.

#### Phase 1 Modifications (Weeks 1-2): Stabilisation

| Element | CUD-Specific Protocol |
|---------|----------------------|
| Withdrawal profile | No validated pharmacological protocol; withdrawal is real but not medically dangerous. Expect: irritability, insomnia, decreased appetite, anxiety, restlessness, vivid dreams |
| Management | Supportive care: sleep hygiene, anxiolytics if indicated, nutritional support, graduated physical activity, psychoeducation that cannabis withdrawal is clinically recognised (DSM-5) |
| Monitoring | Daily clinical review; PHQ-9 if depressive symptoms emerge; appetite and weight tracking |
| Duration | Acute symptoms typically 3-7 days; sleep disturbance and irritability may persist 2-4 weeks |
| Engagement challenge | CUD clients are most likely to minimise their condition; begin MI engagement immediately |

#### Phase 2 Modifications (Weeks 3-6): Therapeutic Foundation

| Element | CUD-Specific Focus |
|---------|-------------------|
| CBT emphasis | Cannabis-specific cognitions: "It's natural, not a real drug," "I can't sleep/eat/relax without it," "Everyone smokes." Strongest CBT effect sizes are for cannabis (PMC10572095) |
| Motivational work | Enhanced MI focus: many CUD clients are in Contemplation or early Preparation; address ambivalence directly; decisional balance exercises |
| Trigger mapping | CUD-specific triggers: peer groups (cannabis use is highly social), music/entertainment contexts, boredom, evening routines, stress relief expectations |
| Psychoeducation | Cannabis and the adolescent brain; amotivational syndrome; impact on memory, learning, and executive function; cannabis and psychosis risk (dose-response relationship) |
| Cognitive rehabilitation | Structured activities targeting executive function recovery: planning exercises, goal-setting, problem-solving tasks |

#### Phase 3-4 Modifications (Weeks 7-12): Deepening & Reintegration

| Element | CUD-Specific Focus |
|---------|-------------------|
| PRPP development | Peer network restructuring (most critical for CUD); alternative stress management and relaxation strategies; evening routine redesign; social skills for cannabis-free socialising |
| Vocational emphasis | Employment/education engagement critical: CUD-related amotivation is the primary barrier to reintegration. Vocational pathway must be activated by Week 7 |
| Aftercare | Peer accountability partnerships; structured evening and weekend activities; ongoing cognitive recovery monitoring (executive function typically improves over 3-6 months of abstinence) |
| Outcome measures | DAST-10 (admission vs discharge); cognitive function assessment; motivational stage (URICA) progression |

### 20.5 Stimulant Use Disorder Pathway

**Prevalence context:** Stimulant use in Nigeria includes cocaine, methamphetamine, and high-dose pharmaceutical stimulants. Stimulant clients often present with acute psychiatric symptoms (paranoia, agitation, psychosis), significant cardiovascular risk, and crash/rebound depression following cessation.

#### Phase 1 Modifications (Weeks 1-2): Stabilisation

| Element | Stimulant-Specific Protocol |
|---------|---------------------------|
| Withdrawal profile | No medically dangerous withdrawal; primary presentation is "crash": hypersomnia, hyperphagia, psychomotor retardation, dysphoria, intense cravings |
| Psychiatric monitoring | Heightened DIP (drug-induced psychosis) risk, especially in first 72-96 hours; assess q4h for paranoid ideation, auditory/visual hallucinations, agitation |
| Management | Rest, nutrition, hydration; antipsychotic if DIP symptoms emerge (per physician); mood monitoring (PHQ-9); suicide risk screening (C-SSRS) -- depression common post-stimulant cessation |
| Cardiovascular | ECG if indicated; blood pressure monitoring; cardiac symptom vigilance |
| Duration | Crash phase: 1-3 days. Withdrawal symptoms (dysphoria, fatigue, anhedonia): 1-4 weeks |

#### Phase 2 Modifications (Weeks 3-6): Therapeutic Foundation

| Element | Stimulant-Specific Focus |
|---------|-------------------------|
| CBT emphasis | Stimulant-specific cognitions: grandiosity, risk-taking justification, "I perform better on it," sexual behaviour under influence. Craving management techniques (cravings are intense but time-limited) |
| Trigger mapping | Stimulant-specific triggers: nightlife, sexual activity, high-pressure work, financial access (binge pattern on payday), specific peer groups |
| Psychoeducation | Dopamine depletion and recovery timeline; anhedonia as a neurobiological symptom (not a character flaw) -- typically improves over 3-6 months; cardiovascular risks; stimulant psychosis risk |
| Mood monitoring | Enhanced PHQ-9 frequency (weekly minimum) during Weeks 3-6; anhedonia is the primary relapse driver for stimulant clients |
| Risk assessment | Enhanced C-SSRS during Weeks 2-6: post-stimulant depression carries suicide risk |

#### Phase 3-4 Modifications (Weeks 7-12): Deepening & Reintegration

| Element | Stimulant-Specific Focus |
|---------|-------------------------|
| PRPP development | Nightlife and social environment avoidance plans; financial accountability structures (consider trusted person managing finances early in recovery); sexual behaviour plan; high-risk situation rehearsal |
| Behavioural activation | Critical for stimulant recovery: structured activities to counteract anhedonia; exercise programme (evidence for exercise improving dopamine recovery); creative pursuits; service activities |
| Aftercare | Extended mood monitoring (anhedonia/depression); NA/CA fellowship placement; structured evening/weekend activities; financial accountability partner |
| Outcome measures | DAST-10 (admission vs discharge); PHQ-9 trend; anhedonia resolution tracking |

### 20.6 Polysubstance Use Pathway

**Prevalence context:** Many HOR clients use multiple substances. The polysubstance pathway applies when a client meets DSM-5 criteria for moderate-to-severe SUD for two or more substances, or when the clinical picture is dominated by combined substance effects rather than a single primary substance.

#### Clinical Approach

| Element | Polysubstance Protocol |
|---------|----------------------|
| Pathway assignment | Primary pathway assigned based on highest clinical severity substance; secondary pathway elements integrated into treatment plan |
| Detoxification | Sequential or concurrent detox management based on withdrawal risk hierarchy: alcohol withdrawal (seizure/DT risk) takes priority, then opioid withdrawal, then other substances |
| Assessment complexity | Full ASI across all substance domains; CIWA-Ar AND COWS if alcohol + opioid co-use; enhanced medical monitoring |
| CBT approach | Address common underlying cognitions across substances (emotional dysregulation, low distress tolerance, impulsivity); substance-specific refusal skills for each substance |
| Trigger mapping | Identify shared triggers (e.g., stress triggers both alcohol and cannabis use) and substance-specific triggers; note "substitution risk" -- switching one substance for another |
| PRPP | Must address ALL substances of use, not just the primary; include substitution risk awareness; comprehensive social network restructuring |
| Pharmacological | Medication interactions must be considered across all withdrawal protocols; physician must coordinate all medication decisions centrally |
| Extended programme | Polysubstance clients are strong candidates for programme extension to 16-26 weeks per NIDA Principle 5 |

## 21. LEVEL-OF-CARE PATHWAYS

### 21.1 Rationale and ASAM Alignment

The ASAM Criteria (Fourth Edition, 2024) define a continuum of care across five major levels. HOR's current programme operates at ASAM Level 3.5 (Clinically Managed High-Intensity Residential). Establishing formal level-of-care pathways creates a connected system where clients can step up or step down based on clinical need, rather than a single programme with a binary admit/discharge decision.

Each level of care below represents a distinct service pathway with its own admission criteria, programme structure, staffing requirements, and transition protocols.

### 21.2 Outpatient Services (ASAM Level 1)

**Purpose:** Provide structured outpatient support for clients who do not require residential placement, clients stepping down from residential care, and clients in the Outpatient Engagement Pathway (Section 4.5) who are building readiness for higher-intensity treatment.

#### Admission Criteria

| Criterion | Standard | Assessment |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| SUD severity | Mild-to-moderate SUD (DSM-5), OR post-residential step-down | ASI, ASSIST |
| Medical stability | No withdrawal risk requiring medical supervision | Physician evaluation |
| Psychiatric stability | Stable; no active suicidal ideation, no psychosis | C-SSRS, clinical interview |
| Living environment | Safe, supportive living situation confirmed | ASAM Dimension 6 assessment |
| Readiness | Contemplation stage or higher on URICA | URICA, MI assessment |
| Support system | At least one identified support person | Clinical interview |

#### Programme Structure

| Component | Specification |
|-----------|--------------|
| Duration | 12-24 weeks (flexible based on clinical progress) |
| Contact frequency | 1-2 sessions per week (individual or group) |
| Session length | 60 minutes (individual), 90 minutes (group) |
| Core interventions | Individual CBT, MI, psychoeducation, relapse prevention |
| Spiritual component | Voluntary: chapel attendance, pastoral counselling available |
| UDS | Monthly, or more frequently if clinically indicated |
| Assessment schedule | URICA, PHQ-9, GAD-7 at intake, monthly, and discharge |
| Operating hours | Weekday daytime and select evenings |

#### Staffing

| Role | Requirement |
|------|-------------|
| Clinical Lead | Oversees outpatient programme; caseload max 30 active clients |
| Counsellor(s) | Trained in CBT and MI; minimum 1 FTE per 15 active clients |
| Physician | Available for consultation; conducts initial medical screening |
| Administrative | Scheduling, records, UDS coordination |

#### Step-Up Criteria (Level 1 to Level 2.1 or 3.5)

A client in outpatient services should be assessed for step-up to a higher level of care when:
- UDS indicates substance use on two or more consecutive tests
- PHQ-9 or GAD-7 scores deteriorate to moderate or above
- Client reports inability to maintain abstinence in current environment
- URICA stage regresses (Action to Contemplation or Precontemplation)
- Safety risk emerges (C-SSRS elevation)
- Living environment becomes unsafe or unsupportive

**Purpose:** Provide a structured, higher-intensity outpatient programme for clients who need more support than weekly outpatient but do not require 24-hour residential supervision. Serves as both a step-up from Level 1 and a step-down from Level 3.5.

#### Admission Criteria

#### Programme Structure

| Component | Specification |
|-----------|--------------|
| Duration | 8-16 weeks |
| Contact frequency | Minimum 9 hours per week (3 days x 3 hours, or 4 days x 2.5 hours) |
| Core interventions | Group CBT (3x weekly, 90 min), individual counselling (weekly, 60 min), psychoeducation (2x weekly, 60 min) |
| Relapse prevention | PRPP development concurrent with programme |
| Spiritual component | Bible study and spiritual formation group (2x weekly, voluntary for non-residential clients) |
| Family engagement | Family psychoeducation session (monthly minimum); family therapy as indicated |
| UDS | Bi-weekly minimum |
| Assessment schedule | URICA, PHQ-9, GAD-7 at intake, bi-weekly, and discharge |

#### Staffing

| Role | Requirement |
|------|-------------|

#### Step-Up Criteria (Level 2.1 to Level 3.5)

| Indicator | Action |
|-----------|--------|

| Indicator | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Sustained abstinence (UDS negative for 4+ consecutive weeks) | Assess for step-down |
| PHQ-9 and GAD-7 stable at mild or minimal | Supports step-down |

### 21.4 Residential Programme (ASAM Level 3.5) -- Current Programme

The residential programme as described in Sections 4-19 of this protocol. No modifications. This section confirms its position within the level-of-care continuum.

**Step-down pathway:** Residential clients who meet graduation criteria (Section 16.3) transition to either Level 2.1 (IOP) or Level 1 (Outpatient) based on clinical assessment, or directly to the Alumni Programme (Section 17) with aftercare monitoring.

**Recommended step-down sequence for high-severity clients:**
1. Residential (12 weeks) → IOP (8-12 weeks) → Outpatient (12-24 weeks) → Alumni
2. Total structured treatment contact: 8-14 months
3. This sequence aligns with McKay's evidence that 12+ months of structured aftercare produces significantly better outcomes

### 21.5 Level-of-Care Transition Protocol

All transitions between levels of care follow a standardised process:

**Step-Up Transitions (Lower → Higher Intensity):**

| Step | Action | Responsible |
|------|--------|-------------|
| 1 | Clinical concern identified and documented | Treating counsellor |
| 2 | Clinical Lead reviews case; confirms step-up indication | Clinical Lead |
| 3 | Physician assessment (medical clearance for residential if applicable) | Program Director / Physician |
| 4 | Client informed; consent obtained; family notified (with consent) | Counsellor + Clinical Lead |
| 5 | Treatment plan updated for new level of care | MDT |
| 6 | Warm handover: outgoing counsellor briefs incoming team; client introduced to new programme | Both teams |
| 7 | First session at new level within 48 hours of transition decision | Receiving programme |

**Step-Down Transitions (Higher → Lower Intensity):**

| Step | Action | Responsible |
|------|--------|-------------|
| 1 | Step-down criteria met; documented in progress notes | Treating counsellor |
| 2 | MDT review and agreement | MDT |
| 3 | Transition plan developed with client (co-authored) | Counsellor + client |
| 4 | Outgoing programme provides clinical summary to receiving programme | Clinical Lead |
| 5 | Client meets receiving counsellor before transition (warm handover) | Both teams |
| 6 | First session at new level within 7 days of residential discharge | Receiving programme |
| 7 | Outgoing counsellor checks in at Day 7 and Day 14 post-transition | Outgoing counsellor |

**Critical principle:** No client should experience a gap in care during transitions. The "warm handover" is non-negotiable -- the client must meet their new treatment team before leaving the current level of care.

## 22. POPULATION-SPECIFIC PATHWAYS

### 22.1 Rationale

The base protocol (Sections 4-19) and substance-specific pathways (Section 20) establish universal and substance-differentiated standards. However, research evidence demonstrates that certain populations require additional modifications to the treatment environment, therapeutic approach, and support services to achieve equitable outcomes.

Population-specific pathways do not replace the base protocol. They modify it in targeted areas while maintaining the universal programme structure, evidence-based interventions, and spiritual formation framework.

### 22.2 Women's Pathway

#### Evidence Base

- Women with SUD show higher rates of co-occurring trauma, depression, and anxiety than men (Greenfield et al., 2007)
- Women are more likely to have experienced intimate partner violence (IPV), sexual assault, and childhood sexual abuse
- Women face unique barriers to treatment entry: childcare responsibilities, stigma (greater for women in most cultures including Nigerian culture), financial dependence on partner
- Women progress from first substance use to dependence faster than men ("telescoping effect")
- Gender-responsive treatment produces better outcomes for women than mixed-gender programmes (SAMHSA, 2009)
- Pregnancy and lactation require specific medical and nutritional protocols

#### Pathway Modifications

### 22.3 Adolescent and Young Adult Pathway (Ages 16-24)

#### Evidence Base

- Adolescent brains are still developing (prefrontal cortex maturation continues to age 25); executive function, impulse control, and risk assessment are neurobiologically immature
- Adolescents show different patterns of substance use: higher rates of cannabis, inhalants, and pharmaceutical misuse; lower rates of alcohol dependence
- Family involvement is more critical for adolescent outcomes than for adults (Liddle, 2004)
- Educational continuity during treatment significantly improves post-treatment outcomes
- Peer influence is the strongest predictor of adolescent substance use (more than family, personality, or availability)
- Adolescents in adult treatment programmes show lower retention rates; age-appropriate programming improves engagement (SAMHSA, 2012)

### 22.4 Dual-Diagnosis Pathway

#### Evidence Base

- NIDA Principle 9: "Many drug-addicted individuals also have other mental disorders." Co-occurring SUD and mental health disorders are the rule, not the exception -- estimates range from 40-65% of SUD treatment populations
- WHO mhGAP (2023) recommends integrated treatment for co-occurring mental health and substance use conditions, particularly in LMIC settings
- Clients with untreated co-occurring disorders show higher rates of treatment dropout, relapse, hospitalisation, and suicide
- Integrated treatment (addressing both conditions simultaneously in one programme) produces superior outcomes to sequential treatment (treating one condition, then the other) or parallel treatment (treating both simultaneously but in separate programmes) -- Drake et al., 2004
- HOR's current exclusion criteria redirect clients with active psychosis or severe psychiatric conditions. The dual-diagnosis pathway enables treatment of clients with stable but ongoing psychiatric conditions alongside SUD

#### Eligibility Criteria

The dual-diagnosis pathway is activated when a client presents with:
- DSM-5 SUD diagnosis (moderate-to-severe) AND
- One or more co-occurring mental health conditions: Major Depressive Disorder, Generalised Anxiety Disorder, Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder, Bipolar Disorder (stable, medicated), Personality Disorder traits (not requiring specialist personality disorder services), Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder

**Exclusion from dual-diagnosis pathway (referral required):**
- Active psychosis (unchanged from base protocol)
- Acute suicidal intent with plan (unchanged)
- Severe eating disorder requiring medical stabilisation
- Conditions requiring inpatient psychiatric care (ASAM Level 4)

#### Treatment Modifications

| Element | Dual-Diagnosis Pathway Modification |
|---------|--------------------------------------|
| Assessment | Enhanced psychiatric assessment at admission: structured clinical interview for DSM-5 diagnoses; medication history review; previous psychiatric treatment history |
| Physician involvement | Weekly psychiatric review (vs monthly in base protocol); medication titration and monitoring |
| Medication management | Psychiatric medication management integrated with SUD treatment: antidepressants, mood stabilisers, anxiolytics (non-benzodiazepine preferred), ADHD medication (non-stimulant preferred) |
| CBT | Integrated dual-focus CBT: sessions address both SUD cognitions and mental health condition-specific cognitions concurrently |
| Individual therapy | Increased frequency: 2x weekly individual sessions (vs 1x in base protocol) |
| Risk monitoring | Enhanced C-SSRS frequency: twice weekly for depressive/PTSD/bipolar clients; PHQ-9 and GAD-7 weekly (vs monthly) |
| Group therapy | Dual-diagnosis psychoeducation group (weekly): understanding the interaction between mental health and substance use; medication adherence; managing symptoms without substances |
| Psychiatric liaison | Established relationship with consulting psychiatrist (Yaba Psychiatric Hospital or private provider); psychiatric consultation available within 48 hours |
| Discharge criteria | Additional criterion: psychiatric stability confirmed by physician; medication plan established for post-discharge; psychiatric follow-up appointment confirmed before discharge |
| Aftercare | Psychiatric follow-up integrated into aftercare plan; medication adherence monitoring; mental health relapse indicators included in PRPP |

#### Staffing Enhancement

| Additional Requirement | Specification |
|------------------------|---------------|
| Consulting psychiatrist | Formal agreement with psychiatrist for weekly clinic and emergency consultation |
| Clinical staff training | All clinical staff trained in dual-diagnosis recognition and basic psychiatric medication knowledge |
| Nursing | Enhanced medication monitoring skills; psychiatric medication side-effect recognition |

### 23.13 SOP-012: Urine Drug Screening (UDS)

**Applies to:** All scheduled and ad-hoc UDS
**Responsible:** Nursing Staff
**Reference:** Section 5.5 (Clinical Assessment Schedule)

| Step | Action | Responsible | Timeframe |
|------|--------|-------------|-----------|
| 1 | Identify client for testing (scheduled monthly for all residents; ad-hoc if clinical suspicion or post-pass) | Nurse / Clinical Lead | Per schedule or as indicated |
| 2 | Inform client of test. UDS is mandatory per admission agreement -- refusal is treated as a positive result | Nurse | At time of test |
| 3 | Specimen collection: client provides sample under indirect observation (staff nearby but not directly watching). Sample labelled with client name, date, time | Nurse | At time of test |
| 4 | Test administered using approved rapid immunoassay kit. Results read at specified time window per kit instructions | Nurse | Per kit instructions |
| 5 | Results documented: substance(s) tested, result (positive/negative) for each, date, time, nurse signature | Nurse | Immediately after reading |
| 6 | Negative result: file in client record. No further action | Nurse | Same day |

| 9 | Document outcome and any treatment plan modifications in client file | Clinical Lead | Within 24 hours |
| 10 | If positive result occurs on return from pass: review pass privileges per behavioural management protocol | Clinical Lead + Program Director | Within 24 hours |
