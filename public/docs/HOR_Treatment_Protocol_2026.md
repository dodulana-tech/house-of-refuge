## 1. PROGRAMME OVERVIEW & PHILOSOPHY

### 1.0 Global Evidence Base

This Treatment Protocol is built on six internationally recognised frameworks and adapted for the Nigerian clinical and cultural context:

**1. NIDA's 13 Principles of Effective Drug Addiction Treatment** (National Institute on Drug Abuse, USA)
The foundational research-based guide established by NIDA after 20+ years of addiction treatment research. HOR's protocol aligns with all 13 principles, notably: no single treatment is appropriate for everyone; treatment needs to be readily available; effective treatment attends to multiple needs of the individual (not just drug abuse); an individual's treatment plan must be assessed continually and modified; treatment does not need to be voluntary to be effective (hence our Outpatient Engagement Pathway); and remaining in treatment for an adequate period of time is critical (our minimum 12-week / 84-day standard, based on evidence that outcomes improve significantly beyond 90 days).

*Source: NIDA, Principles of Drug Addiction Treatment: A Research-Based Guide, Third Edition (nida.nih.gov)*

**2. WHO Mental Health Gap Action Programme (mhGAP) Guidelines** (2023 Update)
The WHO mhGAP intervention guide provides evidence-based recommendations for substance use disorder treatment in low- and middle-income countries. HOR implements mhGAP recommendations for: psychosocial interventions as first-line treatment, structured withdrawal management, and integration of mental health screening (PHQ-9, GAD-7) into substance abuse treatment. The mhGAP framework specifically validates our approach of training non-specialist health workers to deliver evidence-based interventions under clinical supervision.

*Source: WHO mhGAP Guideline for Mental, Neurological and Substance Use Disorders, 2023 (who.int)*

**3. UNODC TREATNET Quality Standards** (United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime)
UNODC's TREATNET training package, developed by an international consortium led by UCLA, sets quality standards for drug dependence treatment in developing countries. Over 10,000 practitioners in 20+ countries have been trained using this framework. HOR's protocol incorporates TREATNET standards for: comprehensive assessment, individualised treatment planning, evidence-based interventions (CBT, MI), community reintegration, and structured aftercare follow-up.

*Source: UNODC TREATNET Training Package (unodc.org/treatment-and-care)*

**4. ASAM Criteria, Fourth Edition (2024)** (American Society of Addiction Medicine)
The most widely used comprehensive set of standards for addiction treatment placement. ASAM's six-dimensional assessment framework informs HOR's admission criteria and treatment matching: (1) Acute Intoxication/Withdrawal Potential, (2) Biomedical Conditions, (3) Emotional/Behavioral/Cognitive Conditions, (4) Readiness to Change, (5) Relapse/Continued Use Potential, (6) Recovery/Living Environment. HOR operates at ASAM Level 3.5 (Clinically Managed High-Intensity Residential Services).

*Source: ASAM Criteria Fourth Edition, 2024 (asam.org)*

**5. Therapeutic Community (TC) Model** (De Leon, Daytop)
The Therapeutic Community approach, pioneered by Dr. George De Leon at Daytop Village, provides the residential community framework for HOR. Research demonstrates that TC participants who stay 90+ days show 9x better outcomes than those who leave early, and 88% of 2-year completers are employed and crime-free. HOR integrates TC principles of "community as method": the therapeutic community itself is the primary instrument of change, through structured daily routines, peer accountability, hierarchical work roles, and group responsibility.

*Source: De Leon, G. "The Therapeutic Community," PMC 2020; Daytop Village Outcomes Research*

**6. Teen Challenge Outcomes Research** (Faith-Based Integration)
Adult & Teen Challenge, the world's largest faith-based residential recovery network (215+ centres), provides the evidence base for Christ-centred recovery programming. Studies show 74% of graduates report no substance use 6 months post-programme, and 87.5% of former marijuana abusers were abstaining 7 years after completion. Research identifies four key elements contributing to recovery: programme structure, relationships with others, experiences of God, and individual initiative. HOR's spiritual formation protocol is modelled on this evidence while maintaining clinical rigour.

*Source: Wilder Research Study of Teen Challenge (2009); Dr. Catherine Hess Study; ACADC Research Review*

### 1.1 Programme Identity

House of Refuge (HOR) is a 24-bed residential drug and alcohol rehabilitation centre located in Lekki, Lagos, Nigeria. Established in 2006 by Freedom Foundation, HOR operates at the intersection of clinical excellence and Christian compassion.

**Capacity:** 24 beds across 4 wings (configurable Male/Female ratio, default 3:1)
**Target Population:** Adults aged 18-65 with moderate-to-severe Substance Use Disorder
**Programme Duration:** 12 weeks (84 days), extendable to 26 weeks where clinically indicated
**Regulatory Registration:** Lagos State Ministry of Health, Lagos State Ministry of Youth, Sport and Social Welfare, NDLEA

### 1.2 Treatment Philosophy

HOR's treatment philosophy rests on four integrated pillars:

1. **Evidence-Based Clinical Practice** - Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT), Motivational Interviewing (MI), and structured Relapse Prevention form the therapeutic backbone. All interventions are grounded in internationally validated approaches with proven efficacy for Substance Use Disorders.

2. **Christ-Centered Spiritual Formation** - The Gospel of Jesus Christ is the ultimate foundation of healing and transformation. Clinical treatment and spiritual formation are not competing priorities - they are complementary pillars of the same healing journey.

3. **Trauma-Informed Care** - Addiction is understood as often rooted in unresolved trauma. All staff interactions are guided by safety, trustworthiness, collaboration, empowerment, and cultural sensitivity.

4. **Reintegration-Focused Outcomes** - Successful rehabilitation is measured not by programme completion but by the client's sustainable, productive, God-honouring reintegration into family, church, and community.

## 2. ADMISSION PROTOCOL

### 2.1 The Willingness Criterion - Primary Gateway

**NON-NEGOTIABLE:** Admission to residential care requires demonstrated, voluntary willingness to participate in recovery.

Willingness is assessed clinically using:
- URICA (University of Rhode Island Change Assessment) Stage of Change evaluation
- Motivational Interviewing-based dialogue
- Clinical interview by Program Director (Medical Doctor)

**Unwilling clients are NOT turned away.** They are enrolled in the Outpatient Engagement Pathway (Section 2.5).

### 2.2 Beneficiary Profile

| Criterion | Specification |
|-----------|---------------|
| Gender | Male & Female |
| Age Range | 18 to 65 years |
| Primary Condition | Substance Use Disorder (moderate to severe) - alcohol, cannabis, opioids, stimulants, inhalants, or polysubstance |
| Psychiatric Status | Stable or manageable. No active psychosis, no acute suicidal/homicidal risk |
| Medical Status | Medically stable or manageable within HOR's 24/7 nursing scope |
| Willingness | Voluntarily seeking treatment; demonstrates insight and readiness |

### 2.3 Five Inclusion Criteria (ALL Must Be Met)

| # | Criterion | Assessment Method |
|---|-----------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Willingness** - voluntary readiness to engage | URICA, MI-based dialogue, clinical interview |
| 2 | **SUD Diagnosis** - meets DSM-5 criteria for moderate-to-severe SUD | ASI (Addiction Severity Index), ASSIST screening |
| 3 | **Medical Stability** - manageable within HOR's scope | Program Director medical evaluation |
| 4 | **Functional Capacity** - can participate in group therapy and counselling | Clinical interview, cognitive screening if indicated |
| 5 | **No Immediate Risk** - no active suicidal ideation with intent/plan, no homicidal intent, no acute psychosis | Standardised safety screening, C-SSRS |

### 2.4 Exclusion Criteria (Immediate Referral Required)

| Condition | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Active Psychosis | Referral to Yaba Psychiatric Hospital. HOR door remains open upon stabilisation |
| Severe Suicidal/Homicidal Risk | Emergency referral to hospital A&E. Family notified immediately |
| Drug-Induced Psychosis (DIP) | Referral for psychiatric stabilisation. Re-assessment upon resolution |
| Antipsychotic Medication Need | Referral to psychiatrist. Re-admission when pharmacologically stable |
| Severe Cognitive Impairment | Referral to neurological/cognitive rehabilitation specialist |
| Active Legal Detention | Liaise with legal authority. Offer outpatient engagement |
| Children/Family Co-habitation | Refer to family-inclusive outpatient services |

**Golden Rule:** Every excluded client leaves with: (1) a written referral, (2) a compassionate conversation, (3) HOR's contact number, (4) basic harm reduction information. Follow-up within 72 hours.

### 2.5 Outpatient Engagement Pathway (Non-Ready Clients)

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Motivational Interviewing | Weekly 45-60 min individual sessions using MI techniques |
| Psychoeducation | Addiction as a brain disorder, recovery process education |
| Family Counselling | With consent - psychoeducation about enabling vs supporting |
| Harm Reduction Guidance | Practical safety information to reduce immediate risk |
| Spiritual Engagement | Voluntary invitation to prayer, pastoral conversation, chapel |
| Reassessment | Every 2-4 weeks via URICA and clinical interview |

### 2.6 Dual Admission Pathways

**Pathway A - Family-Supported Admission:**
- Family consent and active engagement documented
- Financial agreement signed (payment plan available)
- Primary Family Support Person (PFSP) identified and named
- Family participates in minimum 2 therapy sessions + 1 pre-discharge meeting
- Family completes psychoeducation on addiction and enabling behaviour
- Home environment assessed for safety and sobriety-supportiveness

**Pathway B - Community-Supported Admission:**
- Vulnerability and Needs Assessment (VNA) by Social Worker
- Sponsorship, community funding, or HOR Compassion Fund confirmed
- Social Worker assigned as primary case manager (treatment + 12-month aftercare)
- Church/faith community partner identified
- Supported housing referral initiated by Week 8
- Vocational assessment completed before discharge

### 2.7 Eight-Step Admission Process

| Step | Content | Responsible |
|------|---------|-------------|
| 1. Initial Contact | Compassionate engagement; explain programme, expectations, costs | Program Officer / Counsellor |
| 2. Pre-Screening | ASI, ASSIST, URICA administered. Willingness assessed. Eligibility determined | Program Director |
| 3. Clinical Assessment | Full medical evaluation. Psychiatric screening. Psychosocial assessment. Medical tests ordered | Doctor + Nursing Staff |
| 4. Admission Decision | Admit / Refer / Defer decision documented. Pathway activated | Program Director |
| 5. Documentation | 7 consent forms signed (Admission Agreement, Informed Consent, Detox Consent, Confidentiality, Family Confidentiality, Belongings Receipt, Financial Agreement, Rights Charter) | Program Officer + Client |
| 6. Intake & Orientation | Property inspection. Contraband check. Belongings inventory. Bed assignment. Full orientation | House Master + Staff |
| 7. Treatment Planning | Columbia Model treatment plan within 72 hours. Client co-authors goals | Clinical Lead + Counsellor |
| 8. Detoxification | Medical detox under doctor supervision with 24/7 nursing (Weeks 1-2) | Doctor + Nursing Staff |

### 2.8 Pre-Admission Medical Tests (Mandatory within 48 hours)

| Test | Clinical Rationale |
|------|-------------------|
| HIV/AIDS Screening | Baseline status; HIV-positive clients NOT excluded, linked to ART |
| Hepatitis A & B Serology | Co-morbidity assessment; immunisation needs |
| Urine Drug Screen (UDS) | Confirms substances of use; baseline for detox planning |
| Full Blood Count (FBC) | Identifies anaemia, infection, haematological issues |
| Liver Function Tests (LFTs) | Critical for alcohol-dependent clients; medication safety |
| Malaria Parasite Smear | Rules out malaria as confounding factor in withdrawal presentation |
| Widal Test | Rules out typhoid fever (Nigerian clinical context) |
| Chest X-Ray (CXR) | Screens for pulmonary TB, aspiration pneumonia |
| Blood Glucose (Random) | Identifies diabetes/hypoglycaemia for nutritional management |

## 3. CLINICAL ASSESSMENT FRAMEWORK

### 3.1 Assessment Instruments

| Instrument | Purpose | Timing |
|------------|---------|--------|
| **ASI** (Addiction Severity Index) | Comprehensive addiction assessment across 7 domains | Admission |
| **ASSIST** (Alcohol, Smoking and Substance Involvement Screening Test) | Substance-specific risk screening | Admission |
| **URICA** (University of Rhode Island Change Assessment) | Stage of change / readiness assessment | Admission, bi-weekly |
| **C-SSRS** (Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale) | Suicide risk screening (6 items) | Admission, weekly, any concern |
| **CIWA-Ar** (Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment - Alcohol) | Alcohol withdrawal severity (10 items, max 67) | Detox phase, scheduled rounds |
| **COWS** (Clinical Opiate Withdrawal Scale) | Opioid withdrawal severity (11 items, max 47+) | Detox phase, scheduled rounds |
| **AUDIT** (Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test) | Alcohol use severity (10 items, max 40) | Admission + Discharge |
| **DAST-10** (Drug Abuse Screening Test) | Drug abuse severity (10 items, max 10) | Admission + Discharge |
| **PHQ-9** (Patient Health Questionnaire) | Depression screening (9 items, max 27) | Admission + Discharge |
| **GAD-7** (Generalised Anxiety Disorder) | Anxiety screening (7 items, max 21) | Admission + Discharge |

### 3.2 CIWA-Ar Scoring Protocol (Alcohol Withdrawal)

**10 assessment items:** Nausea/vomiting, Tremor, Paroxysmal sweats, Anxiety, Agitation, Tactile disturbances, Auditory disturbances, Visual disturbances, Headache, Orientation/clouding

| Score Range | Severity | Clinical Action |
|-------------|----------|-----------------|
| 0-8 | Mild | Continue monitoring q4h. No medication indicated |
| 8-15 | Moderate | Start withdrawal medication. Monitor q2h |
| 15-20 | Severe | Symptom-triggered benzodiazepine protocol. Monitor q1h |
| >20 | Very Severe / DT Risk | Intensive monitoring. Consider transfer if not stabilising |

### 3.3 COWS Scoring Protocol (Opioid Withdrawal)

**11 assessment items:** Resting pulse rate, Sweating, Restlessness, Pupil size, Bone/joint aches, Runny nose/tearing, GI upset, Tremor, Yawning, Anxiety/irritability, Gooseflesh skin

| Score Range | Severity | Clinical Action |
|-------------|----------|-----------------|
| 0-5 | Minimal | Continue supportive care |
| 5-12 | Mild | Supportive care, comfort medications |
| 13-24 | Moderate | Monitor closely, symptom management |
| 25-36 | Moderately Severe | Active pharmacological management |
| >36 | Severe | Intensive management, consider transfer |

### 3.4 C-SSRS Risk Assessment Protocol

**6 screening questions (all Yes/No):**

1. Wish to be dead
2. Suicidal thoughts (non-specific)
3. Suicidal thoughts with method (without plan)
4. Suicidal intent (without specific plan)
5. Suicidal intent with plan
6. Suicidal behaviour (with timeframe: lifetime / past 3 months / past month / since last visit)

**Additional safety screening:**
- Active psychosis indicators (select from standard list)
- Homicidal ideation (Yes/No)
- Self-harm history (None / Historical / Past 3 months / Current)

| Risk Level | Criteria | Required Action |
|------------|----------|-----------------|
| **Low** | Q1-2 only, no other flags | Continue routine monitoring |
| **Moderate** | Q3-4, or past self-harm | Increase observation, notify Clinical Lead, document |
| **High** | Q5, or Q3-4 with self-harm history | Immediate 1-to-1 supervision, notify Program Director, contact family |
| **Critical** | Q6 (recent), or active psychosis/homicidal ideation | Emergency protocol: refer to Yaba Psychiatric Hospital or nearest A&E |

## 5. DETOXIFICATION PROTOCOL

### 5.1 Scope

Medical detoxification is conducted under physician supervision with 24/7 nursing monitoring during Weeks 1-2. The goal is safe, medically managed withdrawal with symptom relief, not punishment.

### 5.2 Alcohol Detox Protocol

- **Assessment:** CIWA-Ar scoring at admission, then q4h (q2h if score >15, q1h if >20)
- **Medication:** Symptom-triggered benzodiazepine protocol (Chlordiazepoxide or Diazepam)
- **Nutritional Support:** Thiamine 100mg IM/IV x 3-5 days, then oral. Multivitamin. Hydration monitoring
- **Monitoring:** Vital signs q4h minimum. Seizure precautions. DT observation protocol
- **Duration:** Typically 5-7 days. Extended monitoring as clinically indicated
- **Completion Criteria:** CIWA-Ar < 8 for 24 hours. Vital signs stable. No seizure risk

### 5.3 Opioid Detox Protocol

- **Assessment:** COWS scoring at admission, then per clinical schedule
- **Medication:** Supportive symptom management (Loperamide for GI, Clonidine for autonomic symptoms, NSAIDs for pain, Promethazine for nausea). NO opioid agonist therapy at HOR
- **Monitoring:** Vital signs. Hydration. Psychological support
- **Duration:** Typically 7-14 days depending on substance and duration of use
- **Completion Criteria:** COWS < 5 for 24 hours. Vital signs stable. Adequate oral intake

### 5.4 Cannabis / Stimulant Detox Protocol

- **Assessment:** Clinical observation. No validated withdrawal scale required
- **Monitoring:** Mood, sleep, appetite, agitation levels
- **Support:** Symptomatic management (sleep hygiene, anxiolytics if indicated, nutritional support)
- **Duration:** Variable. Typically 3-7 days for acute symptoms

### 5.5 Medication Management Standards

- **No opioid-containing medications** permitted in the facility under any circumstances
- All medications surrendered to nursing staff on admission
- All medications administered exclusively by licensed nursing staff
- Documented in Medication Administration Record (MAR) at each round
- Medication storage: locked cabinet, controlled access, stock reconciliation weekly
- No resident retains any medication (exceptions: nitroglycerin, insulin, seizure medication, asthma medication - doctor-approved only)

## 6. THERAPEUTIC INTERVENTIONS

### 6.1 Treatment Plan Structure (Columbia Model)

Each client's treatment plan follows a structured Problem-Goal-Intervention model:

**Step 1: Problem Identification** (DSM-5 aligned)
- Substance Use Disorder
- Withdrawal Management
- Co-occurring Mental Health
- Relapse Risk
- Family/Relationship Dysfunction
- Housing Instability
- Employment/Vocational Deficit
- Spiritual Disconnection
- Trauma
- Legal Complications

**Step 2: SMART Goals** (Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, Time-bound)
- 3-7 goals per identified problem
- Goals co-authored with client
- Reviewed at MDT (Week 4, Week 8, Pre-discharge)

**Step 3: Interventions** (linked to goals)
- Individual CBT, Group CBT, MI, Psychoeducation, Spiritual Formation, Vocational Training, Family Therapy, Pastoral Counselling

**Step 4: Progress Tracking**
- Goal status: Not Started / In Progress / Completed / Deferred / Modified
- Treatment plan updated at each MDT review
- Client participates in all reviews

### 6.2 Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)

- **Individual:** 60-minute sessions, weekly minimum per client
- **Group:** 90-minute sessions, 3x weekly, max 20 participants
- **Focus:** Thought records, cognitive restructuring, HALT model (Hungry, Angry, Lonely, Tired), trigger mapping, coping strategy toolbox, refusal skills training
- **PRPP Development:** Personal Relapse Prevention Plan drafted in Phase 3, finalised in Phase 4

### 6.3 Motivational Interviewing (MI)

Applied throughout the programme, particularly during:
- Pre-admission screening
- Early engagement (Phase 1-2)
- Resistance or ambivalence about change
- Outpatient Engagement Pathway

Core MI techniques: Open questions, Affirmations, Reflective listening, Summaries (OARS)

### 6.4 Personal Relapse Prevention Plan (PRPP)

Every client must develop a written PRPP as a graduation requirement:

**Trigger Identification** across 5 categories:
1. People (former using friends, dealers, romantic partner)
2. Places (bars, former neighbourhood, certain routes)
3. Feelings (anger, loneliness, boredom, anxiety, shame)
4. Situations (financial pressure, arguments, celebrations, payday)
5. Times (evenings, weekends, holidays, paydays)

**Minimum 5 Active Coping Strategies** (from approved list):
- Deep breathing exercises
- Call sponsor/accountability partner
- Attend NA/AA or church-based recovery group
- Physical exercise
- Prayer and meditation on Scripture
- Journaling
- Contact crisis helpline (09011277600)
- Remove self from triggering environment
- Grounding techniques (5-4-3-2-1)
- Engage in hobby or creative activity
- Talk to trusted family member
- Read Bible / devotional material

**Named Recovery Support Network:**
- Sponsor/accountability partner
- Primary Family Support Person (Pathway A) or Social Worker (Pathway B)
- Church pastor or Life Centre leader
- NA/AA home group or recovery fellowship

## 10. RISK ASSESSMENT & SAFETY PROTOCOL

### 10.1 Routine Risk Screening

| Instrument | Frequency | Responsible |
|------------|-----------|-------------|
| C-SSRS (Suicide Risk) | Admission, weekly, any clinical concern | Clinical staff |
| Psychosis screening | Admission, daily during detox, any concern | Nursing + Clinical |
| Violence risk | Admission, ongoing observation | All staff |
| Self-harm assessment | Admission, weekly during Phases 1-2 | Nursing + Clinical |

### 10.2 Drug-Induced Psychosis (DIP) Response Protocol

1. STAY CALM - Do not argue with delusions
2. ENSURE SAFETY - of client, other residents, and staff
3. NOTIFY nursing staff and Program Director IMMEDIATELY
4. PROVIDE continuous calm 1-to-1 supervision
5. Do NOT leave client unattended until medical assessment complete
6. Program Director initiates IMMEDIATE REFERRAL to Yaba Psychiatric Hospital
7. FAMILY NOTIFICATION
8. DOCUMENT FULLY in client file

### 10.3 Emergency Escalation Pathway

Immediate safety risk → Nurse/Crisis Officer → Clinical Lead → Program Director → Board (if serious)
Safeguarding concern → Designated Safeguarding Lead / Program Director immediately

## 12. BEHAVIOURAL MANAGEMENT PROTOCOL

### 12.1 Philosophy

Discipline at HOR is one of the most powerful therapeutic tools available. It teaches self-governance, delayed gratification, and accountability. It is NEVER punishment or humiliation.

> *"No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it."* - Hebrews 12:11

### 12.2 Three-Tier System

**Tier 1 - Minor Offenses:**
- Examples: Poor hygiene, dormitory cleanliness, lateness, food in rooms, phone abuse, laziness on duties
- Responses: Verbal warning (documented), written reflection exercise, temporary privilege loss

**Tier 2 - Major Offenses:**
- Examples: Substance use on pass, smoking on premises, stealing, property damage, threats, not returning from pass
- Responses: Formal reprimand, extra work assignments, written confession/apology, Bible passage memorisation, loss of all privileges, motivational discipline activated

**Tier 3 - Motivational Discipline:**
- Applied for: Disengagement pattern, spiritual stagnation, consistent failure to progress
- Duration: Minimum 2 weeks, reviewed weekly by Program Director
- Measures: Special work crew, graduation date on hold, loss of all pass/phone, all free time redirected to prayer/Bible study/reflection, weekly evaluation meetings

### 12.3 Dismissal Offenses

- Being drunk or under the influence on premises
- Leaving facility without permission
- Physical assault
- Persistent excessive rule violations
- Refusal to accept disciplinary response
- Sexual misconduct
- Causing severe disunity

### 12.4 Dismissal Procedure

1. Program Director consults Chaplain, informs Executive Director and Board
2. Full documentation of violations
3. Compassionate private conversation explaining reasons
4. Time to pack belongings
5. Contact family for transportation
6. Prayer offered. Referrals provided
7. Transport arranged if needed
8. **NO CLIENT LEAVES WITHOUT A SAFETY PLAN**

## 13. DISCHARGE & GRADUATION PROTOCOL

### 13.1 Four Discharge Types

| Type | Description | Protocol |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Type 1: Planned (Graduation)** | Completes programme, meets all 6 criteria | Full graduation ceremony |
| **Type 2: Administrative** | Dismissed for serious behavioural violation | 30-day minimum before re-admission considered |
| **Type 3: Clinical** | Needs exceed HOR scope (psychiatric/medical) | Written referral, accompanied transport |
| **Type 4: Self-Discharge (AMA)** | Voluntary departure before completion | AMA form signed, harm reduction counselling |

### 13.2 Six Graduation Criteria (ALL Must Be Demonstrated)

| # | Criterion | Standard | Assessment Source |
|---|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Clinical Stability** | No withdrawal, stable vitals, no psychiatric symptoms, sustained abstinence | Nursing records + Doctor sign-off |
| 2 | **Behavioural Compliance** | Rule compliance last 4 weeks, positive community participation | Counsellor report |
| 3 | **Psychological Readiness** | Clear insight into addiction, articulates PRPP verbally and written | Counsellor + PRPP review |
| 4 | **Relapse Prevention Competence** | Written PRPP complete, 5+ coping strategies, named support network | PRPP document |
| 5 | **Spiritual Formation** | Authentic Christian growth, values transformation (not mere compliance) | Chaplain report |
| 6 | **Reintegration Readiness** | Confirmed safe placement, family/community meeting done, aftercare finalised | Social Worker / Counsellor |

### 13.3 Non-Negotiable Discharge Standard

Regardless of discharge type, EVERY client leaves with:
- Basic safety plan (emergency contacts, crisis line, nearest health facility)
- Harm reduction information (if self-discharging or administrative)
- Written referral to appropriate community services
- HOR's contact number
- Prayer and a compassionate farewell

### 13.4 Re-Admission Policy

HOR operates an open-door re-admission policy. Relapse is a clinical event, not a moral failure.

| Criterion | Standard |
|-----------|----------|
| Willingness re-established | Full assessment conducted |
| Relapse reflection | Articulate what contributed and what will differ |
| Exclusion criteria absent | All original criteria still apply |
| Administrative discharge gap | Minimum 30 days; demonstrate changed attitude |
| Modified treatment plan | New individualised plan, not a repeat |
| Re-admission interview | Conducted by Program Director |
